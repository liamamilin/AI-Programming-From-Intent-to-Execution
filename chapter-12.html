13  Goal Compression

13.1 From Complexity to Clarity

In our exploration of Possibility Space, we identified Goal Space as the first layer—the set of all possible outcomes (Z) we might aim for. We discovered that many people struggle with “I don’t know what to build” precisely because they cannot see the Goal Space clearly.

But even when we can see the Goal Space, we face another challenge: the goals we discover are often vague, complex, and unstructured. They capture an intuition or aspiration but lack the precision necessary for system development.

Consider these common initial goals:

  • “Build something useful”
  • “Improve user experience”
  • “Make it smarter”
  • “Enhance productivity”

These statements feel meaningful and inspiring. They communicate a general direction and aspiration. But they are not actionable. They cannot guide system development effectively.

To build systems, we must do more than identify possible goals from the Goal Space. We must transform them. They must move from vague aspirations to precise, testable definitions.

Goal Compression is the process of turning vague intentions into precise, testable definitions.

13.2 What is Goal Compression?

Goal compression is the act of:

Reducing ambiguity while preserving intent

It does not simplify the goal by removing meaning or value. Instead, it simplifies by:

  • Eliminating vagueness—making implicit assumptions explicit
  • Defining boundaries—specifying what is included and excluded
  • Making outcomes observable—defining how success can be measured

13.2.1 Key Idea

A compressed goal is:

  • Clear—unambiguous and specific
  • Bounded—has defined scope and limits
  • Verifiable—can be tested against objective criteria

13.3 Why Compression is Necessary

Without goal compression, the entire system development process is compromised:

  • Intent remains unclear—the team doesn’t agree on what they’re building
  • Spec becomes inconsistent—requirements conflict or contradict each other
  • Instruction becomes unstable—execution produces unreliable results
  • Evaluation becomes subjective—there’s no way to measure success

In other words:

The entire system becomes unreliable and difficult to manage.

13.4 From Vague to Verifiable

Goal compression transforms goals along a spectrum, moving them from vague inspiration to precise, testable specification:

Vague → Clear → Structured → Verifiable

13.4.1 Example Transformation

Consider the goal: “Build a smart assistant”

Vague: > “Build a smart assistant.”

This captures the aspiration but provides no guidance for implementation.

Clear: > “Build an assistant that answers user questions.”

Better—we now know what the system should do. But still not specific enough.

Structured: > “Build a system that answers user questions using retrieved knowledge.”

Good—we now understand the mechanism. But still missing important details.

Verifiable: > “Given a user query, the system retrieves relevant documents from a knowledge base and generates an answer grounded in those documents, with a response time under 2 seconds and an accuracy of at least 80% as measured by human evaluators.”

Now we have a goal that can guide implementation and be tested for success.

The final stage allows evaluation and systematic improvement.

13.5 The Core Dimensions of a Clear Goal

A well-compressed goal typically defines five essential dimensions. These dimensions provide a complete picture of what the goal entails.

13.5.1 1. Objective

What outcome is expected? What is the primary purpose?

This defines the “what” of the goal—the core result that should be achieved.

Example: > “Answer user questions”

13.5.2 2. Object

What is being acted upon? What entity or domain is the target?

This defines the scope and focus of the effort.

Example: > “User queries”

13.5.3 3. Context

Under what conditions does the goal apply? What is the environment or situation?

This provides the boundaries within which the system must operate.

Example: > “Within a web application with 1000+ concurrent users”

13.5.4 4. Constraints

What limits exist? What boundaries must be respected?

Constraints define both what must be done and what must not be done.

Example: > “Response time < 2 seconds, 95% accuracy”

13.5.5 5. Success Criteria

How do we measure success? What indicates that the goal has been achieved?

Success criteria provide the basis for evaluation and improvement.

Example: > “Answers are relevant and grounded in retrieved documents, as measured by human evaluation”

Without these dimensions, a goal is incomplete and cannot effectively guide system development.

13.6 Compression as Reduction of Degrees of Freedom

Another way to understand goal compression is in terms of reducing the number of possible interpretations.

13.6.1 Before Compression

  • Many possible meanings—the goal can be interpreted in multiple ways
  • Many possible outputs—the system could produce many different results
  • High uncertainty—it’s unclear what the right approach is

13.6.2 After Compression

  • Narrowed interpretation—the goal has a single, clear meaning
  • Constrained outputs—the system has limited freedom in what it produces
  • Reduced uncertainty—the path forward is clear

This is critical for systems:

  • Systems cannot choose among infinite interpretations—they need explicit guidance
  • They require bounded spaces to operate effectively
  • Ambiguity leads to unpredictable and unreliable behavior

13.7 Compression vs Over-Specification

There is an important distinction between:

  • Compressing a goal—removing ambiguity while preserving intent
  • Over-constraining a goal—adding unnecessary restrictions that limit flexibility

13.7.1 Compression

  • Removes ambiguity—makes the goal clear and specific
  • Defines boundaries—specifies what is included and excluded
  • Preserves intent—maintains the original purpose and value

13.7.2 Over-Specification

  • Fixes too many details—specifies implementation choices unnecessarily
  • Limits flexibility—reduces the system’s ability to adapt
  • Adds constraints without purpose—creates unnecessary complexity

13.7.3 Example

Compressed: > “Generate a summary under 100 words.”

This specifies a clear constraint without dictating the content or style.

Over-specified: > “Use exactly 3 sentences, each with 12–15 words, using formal academic tone, beginning with a clear topic sentence.”

This specifies too many details and unnecessarily limits the system’s flexibility.

Good compression clarifies. Over-specification restricts unnecessarily.

13.8 Techniques for Goal Compression

Several practical techniques can help compress goals effectively:

13.8.1 1. Ask “What Does Success Look Like?”

Force yourself to define observable, measurable outcomes:

  • What will we see when the goal is achieved?
  • What will be different?
  • How will we know it works?

This forces specificity and creates evaluation criteria.

13.8.2 2. Identify the Transformation

Define the change the system should create:

Current State → Desired State

What is the situation now? What should it be after the system is deployed?

13.8.3 3. Remove Ambiguous Terms

Replace subjective or vague terms with specific, measurable ones:

  • “better” → “20% faster”
  • “fast” → “under 2 seconds”
  • “smart” → “accurate on 95% of test cases”

13.8.4 4. Add Constraints

Define limits explicitly:

  • Time constraints—“must complete within X seconds”
  • Format constraints—“must output JSON”
  • Scope constraints—“only process documents under 10MB”

13.8.5 5. Test for Interpretability

Ask yourself:

Can another person (or system) execute this without guessing?

If the answer is no, the goal needs more compression.

13.9 Common Mistakes

13.9.1 1. Staying at the Idea Level

  • Never defining details—remaining abstract and vague
  • Remaining inspirational—failing to become actionable
  • Moving to implementation prematurely—without clarifying the goal

13.9.2 2. Mixing Goals with Implementation

  • Jumping to tools or frameworks—before clarifying what’s needed
  • Focusing on how to build—before understanding what to build
  • Losing focus on outcome—getting distracted by implementation details

13.9.3 3. Ignoring Evaluation

  • No way to verify success—no criteria for measurement
  • Can’t assess improvement—no baseline for comparison
  • Subjective assessment—relying on gut feel rather than data

13.9.4 4. Overcomplicating the Goal

  • Adding unnecessary details—cluttering the goal with irrelevant information
  • Reducing clarity—too much information obscures the core objective
  • Making it hard to remember—complex goals are difficult to communicate

13.10 Goal Compression as a Skill

Goal compression improves with practice. It is not a natural talent but a learnable skill.

It requires:

  • Clear thinking—the ability to distill complex ideas to their essence
  • Precision in language—choosing words carefully and specifically
  • Awareness of system behavior—understanding what systems need to execute effectively

The more you practice, the faster you can:

  • Move from vague to precise—compressing goals rapidly
  • Define systems effectively—creating actionable specifications
  • Communicate clearly—ensuring everyone understands the goal

13.11 Final Insight

Goal compression is the bridge between intention and specification. Without it:

  • Intent remains unusable—ideas cannot be realized
  • Spec cannot be formed—requirements cannot be specified
  • Systems cannot be built—there’s no clear direction

With it:

  • Intent becomes actionable—ideas can guide development
  • Spec becomes possible—requirements can be specified
  • Systems become buildable—there’s a clear path forward

You cannot build what you cannot define.

13.12 Closing Statement

In the LLM era, the ability to compress goals is a core capability. It is the foundation of effective system development.

The clearer the goal, the more reliable the system.

13.13 Key Insight

Goal compression transforms vague intentions into precise, testable definitions. A well-compressed goal specifies objective, object, context, constraints, and success criteria.

13.14 Action Hint

Practice goal compression on your current projects. Take your existing goals and run them through the compression process: Vague → Clear → Structured → Verifiable. Ask yourself: “Is this specific enough that someone else could execute it without guessing?”
