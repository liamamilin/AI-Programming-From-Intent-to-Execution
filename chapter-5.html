6  Intent: Defining What to Do

6.1 The Starting Point of All Systems

Every system begins with an intention—a desire to create something, solve a problem, or achieve an outcome. Before any specification is written, before any instruction is executed, before any code is generated, there is a fundamental question:

What are we trying to do?

This question seems simple, almost trivial. Yet it is often the most difficult to answer effectively. In the LLM era, where execution has become easy and automated, the quality of the outcome depends almost entirely on the clarity and precision of this initial definition.

Intent is the foundation of everything that follows.

6.2 What is Intent?

Intent is the human-level definition of a desired outcome. It represents the starting point of any system-building effort—the seed from which all else grows.

Intent describes:

  • What change we want to create—the difference between the current state and desired state
  • What problem we want to solve—the issue or challenge that motivates the effort
  • What result we expect to observe—the tangible outcomes that would indicate success

Intent is not code, and it is not yet a specification. It is:

  • Conceptual—it exists in the realm of ideas and understanding
  • Context-dependent—it derives meaning from the situation and environment
  • Often incomplete at first—it starts as a vague idea that needs refinement

Intent exists in human cognition, not in machine logic. It is the bridge between human desire and system capability.

6.3 Intent as a Transformation

A useful way to understand intent is as a transformation—a movement from one state to another:

Current State → Desired State

This framing makes intent concrete and operational. Intent answers two fundamental questions:

  • Where are we now?—the current situation, problem, or limitation
  • Where do we want to go?—the desired outcome, solution, or capability

For example, consider the intent to build a customer service chatbot:

  • Current state: Customers must wait hours for email responses to simple questions
  • Desired state: Customers receive immediate, accurate answers to common questions 24/7

This transformation framing makes intent specific and actionable. It provides a clear direction for the system-building effort.

6.4 The Minimum Structure of Intent

Although intent often begins as a vague idea, it must eventually be structured in order to guide system development effectively. A well-formed intent should clarify several essential elements:

6.4.1 1. Objective

What outcome is desired? What is the primary goal?

This is the “what” of intent—the core objective that the system should achieve.

6.4.2 2. Object

What is the action applied to? What entity or domain is being affected?

This defines the scope and focus of the effort.

6.4.3 3. Context

Under what conditions does the intent apply? What is the environment or situation?

Context provides the boundaries within which the system must operate.

6.4.4 4. Constraints

What limits exist? What boundaries must be respected?

Constraints define what the system cannot do, as well as what it must do.

6.4.5 5. Success Criteria

How do we know when the intent has been achieved? What indicates success?

Success criteria provide a way to measure whether the system has accomplished its goal.

Without these elements, intent remains ambiguous and cannot effectively guide system development.

6.5 The Illusion of Clarity

A common and dangerous mistake is believing that an idea is clear simply because it feels clear in our minds. Our intuition often creates an illusion of understanding that does not survive scrutiny.

For example, consider this common intent:

“Build a smart assistant.”

This statement appears meaningful at first glance. It seems to communicate a clear goal. Yet on closer examination, it lacks:

  • Defined behavior—what should the assistant actually do?
  • Scope—what domain or problem does it address?
  • Constraints—what are the limitations on its capabilities?
  • Evaluation criteria—how do we know if it’s working?

It creates an illusion of understanding while providing no real guidance for implementation.

Clarity in thought must be tested through expression.

If you cannot specify:

  • What it does—the concrete functions it performs
  • For whom—the intended users or stakeholders
  • Under what conditions—the context and environment
  • What counts as success—the measurable outcomes

Then the intent is not yet clear enough to guide system development.

6.6 Why Most People “Haven’t Thought It Through”

The difficulty of formulating clear intent is not primarily technical—it is cognitive. Several common factors contribute to this difficulty:

6.6.1 1. Lack of Exposure to Possibility

If you do not know what is possible, you cannot define what you want with precision. Your intent is bounded by your awareness of what can be built.

  • Limited experience → limited imagination of what’s possible
  • No pattern awareness → vague goals that don’t leverage known capabilities
  • ** unfamiliarity with AI capabilities** → underestimation or overestimation of what can be achieved

6.6.2 2. Mixing Intent with Implementation

People often jump directly to implementation concerns without first clarifying the intent:

  • Tools—“I want to use React and Node.js”
  • Frameworks—“I need to use LangChain”
  • Code—“I’ll write a function that…”

Instead of first asking:

“What exactly should exist?”

This leads to premature decisions and poor system design. The tool should serve the intent, not the other way around.

6.6.3 3. Ambiguity Tolerance

Humans are comfortable with vague ideas and incomplete specifications. We can say things like:

  • “Make it better”
  • “Improve performance”
  • “Make it user-friendly”

And feel that we’ve communicated something meaningful. But machines cannot operate on ambiguity. They need precision.

What is acceptable for humans is insufficient for systems.

6.6.4 4. Missing Evaluation Criteria

Many intents fail because they lack a clear definition of success. Without evaluation criteria:

  • There is no way to know if the system works
  • Iteration becomes random and directionless
  • Results feel inconsistent and unpredictable

Success criteria provide the feedback loop necessary for systematic improvement.

6.7 Intent vs Idea

It is important to distinguish between an idea and an intent. These are related but distinct concepts:

6.7.1 Idea

  • Vague—lacks specificity and precision
  • Inspirational—captures a general direction or aspiration
  • Open-ended—does not constrain possibilities

Example: > “An AI tool for productivity”

An idea is a starting point, but it is not yet actionable.

6.7.2 Intent

  • Structured—has clear components and boundaries
  • Bounded—defines what is included and what is excluded
  • Testable—can be evaluated against specific criteria

Example: > “A system that summarizes daily meeting transcripts into actionable bullet points within 5 seconds, with a maximum of 10 bullet points per meeting.”

Ideas inspire. Intent defines.

The transformation from idea to intent is the process of adding structure, precision, and testability to an initial inspiration.

6.8 Intent as a Skill

Intent is not a one-time decision or a natural talent. It is a skill that can be developed and improved through practice.

It involves:

  • Observing real problems—developing sensitivity to genuine needs and pain points
  • Understanding system capabilities—knowing what tools and technologies can achieve
  • Structuring goals clearly—organizing complex desires into coherent objectives
  • Reducing ambiguity—eliminating vagueness and making implicit assumptions explicit

The better your intent-formulation skills, the more effective every subsequent step in the system-building process becomes. A well-formed intent makes spec creation easier, instruction design more straightforward, and evaluation more meaningful.

6.9 The Cost of Weak Intent

If intent is unclear or poorly structured, the consequences propagate through the entire system development process:

  • Spec becomes inconsistent—the specification lacks coherence and clarity
  • Instruction becomes unstable—execution protocols produce unreliable results
  • Execution becomes unreliable—outputs vary unpredictably
  • Evaluation becomes subjective—there’s no clear standard for success

The entire system degrades. Weak intent is not a small problem—it is a fundamental flaw that undermines everything that follows.

Weak intent propagates errors through the entire pipeline.

6.10 Strengthening Intent

To improve intent, one must engage in deliberate practice. This is not a technical exercise—it is a thinking discipline.

Key strategies include:

  • Expand awareness of possibilities—study existing systems, explore new technologies, understand what’s feasible
  • Practice structuring goals—use frameworks and templates to organize thinking
  • Define explicit success criteria—make it clear how you’ll know when you’ve succeeded
  • Separate intention from implementation—focus first on what you want, not how you’ll build it

These practices build the cognitive muscles necessary for effective intent formulation.

6.11 Final Insight

Intent is where human cognition meets system design. It is the point where:

  • Reality is interpreted—understanding the current situation and needs
  • Goals are defined—articulating what should change and why
  • Systems begin to take shape—providing the direction for all subsequent work

Intent is not a preliminary step to be rushed through. It is the foundation upon which everything else depends.

If you cannot define what you want, no system can reliably produce it.

6.12 Closing Statement

In the LLM era, the most powerful developers are not those who can execute the fastest or write the most code. They are those who can define the clearest intent.

The quality of your systems begins with the clarity of your intent.

6.13 Key Insight

Intent is the foundation of all systems. A well-formed intent must specify objective, object, context, constraints, and success criteria. The ability to formulate clear intent is a learnable skill.

6.14 Action Hint

Before your next project, write down your intent using this structure: “I want to [objective] for [object] in [context], respecting [constraints], with success measured by [success criteria].” Revise it until it’s precise and testable.
